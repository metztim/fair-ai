## Executive Summary

The evidence strongly suggests that **building a credible AI ethics ratings organization from Thailand is viable but comes with significant trade-offs**. Ben Thompson's success with Stratechery demonstrates that a Thailand-based analyst can build tremendous influence over US tech companies, but his 2025 relocation to the US after 11+ years reveals the inherent limitations of remote operation.

**Key Findings:**

1. **Remote Success is Proven**: Thompson built one of tech's most influential voices from Taiwan, interviewing CEOs of major companies and shaping industry conversation—proving that physical presence in Silicon Valley is not required for credibility or access.

2. **But Limitations Are Real**: Thompson's own words reveal that "being on the opposite side of the world was definitely limiting in a lot of ways," leading him to relocate to optimize for new opportunities beyond the one-to-many newsletter format.

3. **The Thailand Advantage**: Operating costs are 60% lower than San Francisco, providing significant runway extension. The time zone enables content production while US audiences sleep. Geographic independence can enhance the perception of unbiased reporting.

4. **Critical Trade-offs**: Time zone friction for real-time engagement, reduced ability to attend key conferences and cultivate insider sources, potential perception challenges for regulatory influence work, and legal/liability exposure considerations.

5. **The Hybrid Path**: A minimum viable US presence strategy (3-4 strategic trips annually, remote team members in key locations, virtual engagement infrastructure) appears to offer the best balance for the launch phase.

**Recommendation**: **CONDITIONAL GO with Hybrid Strategy**. Launch from Thailand with a clear plan for strategic US engagement, understanding that future scaling may require either relocation or establishing a US operational presence. The Consumer Reports model (nonprofit, subscription-based, no advertising) is proven and compatible with remote operation, but regulatory influence work will require more US presence than pure media would.

## 1. Ben Thompson Case Study: Building Stratechery from Taiwan

### Background & Success Metrics

Ben Thompson founded Stratechery in 2013 while living in Taipei, Taiwan, where his wife is from. He grew it from "some guy in Taiwan with no access" to a powerhouse that shapes technology industry conversation at the highest levels.

**Scale Achieved:**
- 6+ million combined print and digital subscribers (2017 figures: 3.6M print, 2.9M digital)
- ~$3 million in annual revenue by 2020
- Interviews with Mark Zuckerberg, Jensen Huang, Satya Nadella, Sundar Pichai, and other Fortune 500 CEOs
- Created "Aggregation Theory," called "the most important business framework developed in the last 20 years"
- Inspired Substack's entire business model (seed pitch was "Stratechery-in-a-box")

**Business Model:**
- Pure subscription model ($30/year for magazine, separate digital tier)
- No advertising, maintaining independence
- Freemium structure (free articles + paid Daily Updates)
- One-person operation for years before scaling

### How He Overcame Distance Challenges

**1. Time Zone as Advantage:**
Thompson consistently cited the time zone (13 hours ahead of US East Coast) as his "biggest advantage." He could:
- Write analysis overnight while Americans slept
- Publish early morning US time when audiences check email
- Provide next-day analysis with time to process news thoroughly
- Cover semiconductor industry with direct access to TSMC headquarters in Taiwan

**2. Asynchronous Communication:**
- Built influence through written analysis, not real-time conversation
- Email newsletter format doesn't require physical presence
- Subscribers email feedback; Thompson responds to best insights
- Cultivated a feedback mechanism that didn't require face-to-face interaction

**3. Selective Travel:**
Thompson's approach to travel and speaking:
- Occasionally agrees to speaking engagements for both public and private events
- Does NOT speak for companies he covers on Stratechery (maintains independence)
- Pays for his own travel and expenses when attending company events
- No consulting or speaking arrangements with companies he covers (as of 2016)

**Specific travel frequency not publicly documented**, but his selective approach suggests he prioritized quality over quantity.

**4. Quality Over Access:**
Rather than relying on insider scoops, Thompson built influence through:
- Superior analytical frameworks (Aggregation Theory, etc.)
- Deep understanding of business models and strategy
- Willingness to acknowledge mistakes publicly
- Consistent, daily publication schedule
- Long-form thinking that transcended breaking news

### Why He Eventually Relocated to the US (2025)

In August 2025, Thompson moved back to the United States. His personal update reveals the nuanced reality:

**Primary Reason: Family**
- Daughter graduated high school, attending US college
- Son's education considerations

**Secondary Considerations:**

1. **Geographic Limitations Acknowledged:**
   - "Being on the opposite side of the world was definitely limiting in a lot of ways"
   - The move represents "a new chapter for Stratechery Plus"
   - Question: "If Taiwan was optimized for a one-to-many email Update, what can I optimize for by being in the U.S.?"
   - Implies he sees new formats/opportunities requiring US presence

2. **Taiwan Security (minor factor):**
   - "Situation in Taiwan is complicated and somewhat riskier than it has been for decades"
   - Does NOT believe military conflict is imminent or inevitable
   - Retains a home in Taiwan, anticipates spending time there

3. **Time Zone Trade-off:**
   - "It has been weird to lose the sensation of living a day in the future"
   - Maintains that the time zone was his "biggest advantage Taiwan afforded"
   - Suggests new opportunities outweigh this loss

### Key Lessons for AI Watchdog Venture

**What Thompson Proved Possible from Remote Location:**
- Building credibility without Silicon Valley proximity
- Securing interviews with the most powerful tech executives
- Shaping industry conversation and thought leadership
- Creating a multi-million dollar subscription business
- Maintaining editorial independence through business model design

**What Required (or Would Benefit from) US Presence:**
- "Optimizing" beyond one-to-many email format (podcasts, events, real-time engagement?)
- New revenue opportunities or content formats
- Deeper relationship cultivation beyond transactional interviews
- Family/life considerations that ultimately drove relocation

**Critical Distinction for AI Watchdog:**
Thompson built an **analysis and commentary business**. An AI ethics ratings organization will require:
- Active testing and evaluation (can be done remotely)
- Regulatory engagement (harder from abroad)
- B2B enterprise sales if pursuing dual revenue stream (manageable remotely but friction exists)
- Media relationships for amplification (very feasible remotely)
- Adversarial relationships with rated companies (distance could be advantage or disadvantage)

**Bottom Line:** Thompson's 11-year run proves remote viability, but his relocation suggests ceiling effects for certain types of engagement and growth.

## 3. What Actually Requires US Presence?

Analysis of functional requirements for an AI ethics ratings organization:

### Regulatory Engagement: MIXED (Can influence remotely, but friction exists)

**Can Be Done Remotely:**
- Written comment submissions to regulatory agencies (FCC, FTC, EU AI Act consultations)
- Virtual testimony at hearings (increasingly common post-COVID)
- Publishing policy briefs and research reports
- Coalition building with US-based advocacy organizations
- Media appearances raising policy issues

**Benefits from US Presence:**
- Hallway conversations at DC events where real policy gets shaped
- In-person relationship building with Congressional staffers
- Participation in closed-door stakeholder meetings
- Credibility perception ("are they serious or just offshore critics?")
- Ability to respond quickly to time-sensitive policy windows

**International Precedents:**
Foreign lobbying is common, but requires registration under FARA (Foreign Agents Registration Act). In 2022-2023, FARA registrants reported $14.3 million in political contributions and 130,000 political activities. However, most successful international policy influence uses **Washington DC-based firms as representatives**.

**Implication for AI Watchdog:**
- Launch phase: Remote policy engagement is viable (written submissions, virtual testimony)
- Growth phase: Consider DC-based policy director or partnership with US advocacy org
- Tim's Dutch citizenship is advantage (EU perspective on AI Act) but requires FARA awareness if formal lobbying

**Risk Assessment:**
State-level FARA laws ("Baby FARA" bills) passed in 5 states in 2025, creating new disclosure requirements. An AI watchdog should structure to avoid triggering these (focus on research/journalism, not lobbying).

### Media Relationships: HIGH VIABILITY REMOTELY

**Evidence of Remote Success:**
- Journalists primarily work via email, phone, video calls
- Press releases and reports can be distributed electronically
- Embargo systems work globally
- Media interviews increasingly done via Zoom/Skype

**Ben Thompson Example:**
Major media outlets regularly cite Stratechery despite his Taiwan location. He became the source, rather than needing to pitch media.

**Consumer Reports Model:**
CR is primarily cited through its reports and ratings, not through personal relationships. A methodologically rigorous AI ratings system would function similarly.

**Best Practices:**
- Build email list of tech reporters covering AI (100+ names easily findable)
- Issue regular research reports with clear media hooks
- Make spokespeople available across time zones (challenge, but manageable)
- Cultivate 2-3 "go-to" journalists at NYT, WSJ, Bloomberg, Reuters

**Time Zone Consideration:**
Thailand is 12-15 hours ahead of US time zones. This means:
- US morning news cycle happens during Thailand evening (doable)
- US afternoon (2-5pm ET) is Thailand midnight-3am (challenging for live hits)
- Possible solution: Pre-record video statements, provide written quotes, schedule morning ET interviews

**Verdict:** 80% effectiveness remotely with good systems, 20% friction for real-time media opportunities.

### Source Cultivation (AI Company Insiders): MODERATE CHALLENGE

**Nature of AI Watchdog Relationships:**
Unlike typical tech journalism (which cultivates sources for scoops), an AI ethics ratings organization has **adversarial relationships** with companies being rated. This changes the dynamic significantly.

**Less Critical Than Traditional Journalism:**
- Ratings based on published information, testing, public documentation
- Not reliant on leaks or insider scoops
- Transparency and methodology matter more than exclusive access
- Consumer Reports model: Companies cooperate to provide products for testing, or products are purchased anonymously

**Where Insider Access Helps:**
- Understanding internal AI safety practices
- Early awareness of new model releases for testing
- Whistleblower cultivation (safety concerns, rushed deployments)
- Technical deep-dives requiring explanation from engineers

**Remote Viability:**
- Whistleblowers often prefer digital communication (anonymity, security)
- Technical conversations happen via video call
- Document sharing works remotely
- **Being outside Silicon Valley may increase perceived independence for potential sources**

**Signal Hire's Research on Tech Journalists:**
"Access to inside sources at major tech companies is a plus, along with the ability to obtain or review internal documents. This is a position for a seasoned journalist who is confident they can produce groundbreaking stories regularly."

However, this describes traditional journalism, not a ratings organization. Consumer Reports doesn't rely on insider sources—it relies on **rigorous methodology**.

**Verdict:** Less critical for ratings model; potential advantage of perceived independence from geographic distance.

**Must-Attend Events for AI Watchdog (Annual):**

   - Closer to Thailand than US conferences

### Enterprise Sales (B2B Revenue Stream): MANAGEABLE WITH SYSTEMS

**Relevant if AI Watchdog Pursues Dual Model:**
- Consumer subscriptions (like Consumer Reports)
- Enterprise licensing (companies buying ratings data, API access, white-labeled reports)

**Remote B2B Sales Viability:**
Modern B2B sales is increasingly remote:
- SaaS companies operate globally with remote sales teams
- Video calls are standard for sales process
- Enterprise buyers care about product value, not seller location
- Proof: GitLab (fully remote) built $1B+ revenue with distributed sales team

**Challenges from Thailand:**
- Time zone friction for live demos/calls (solvable with flexible hours)
- Travel for final contract negotiations (major enterprise deals often require in-person closing)
- Perception: "Are they a serious vendor or a side project?" (mitigated by professional execution)

**Solutions:**
- Hire US-based sales director if pursuing enterprise revenue aggressively
- Tim focuses on product/research; sales lead manages US time zone engagement
- Use async sales tools (Loom videos, detailed proposals, recorded demos)

**Consumer Reports Model:**
CR does NOT pursue aggressive B2B sales—it's membership-based nonprofit. If AI watchdog follows this model, enterprise sales requirements are minimal.

**Verdict:** If pursuing significant B2B revenue, US-based sales presence recommended. If membership/consumer model, not critical.

## 5. Thailand-Specific Disadvantages

### Time Zone for US Coverage (When Real-Time Matters)

**Thailand Time Zones:**
- Bangkok: GMT+7
- San Francisco: GMT-8 (15 hours behind)
- New York: GMT-5 (12 hours behind)

**Challenging Scenarios:**

1. **Live Media Appearances:**
   - US morning shows (7-9am ET) = Thailand 7-9pm (doable)
   - US afternoon news (2-5pm ET) = Thailand 2-5am (very difficult)
   - Solution: Pre-recorded segments, written statements, strategic scheduling

2. **Breaking News Response:**
   - Major AI incident happens 3pm US time
   - Thailand time: 3am next day
   - Competitors issue statements within hours; AI watchdog responds 12+ hours later
   - **Significance:** For a ratings org (not breaking news), this matters less than for daily journalism

3. **Synchronous Collaboration:**
   - US team member working 9am-5pm PT = Thailand midnight-8am
   - Real-time calls require someone working very early/late
   - Solution: Hire team members across time zones, use async communication

4. **Conference Calls with US Stakeholders:**
   - US business hours are Thailand night
   - Sustainable for occasional calls, exhausting if frequent
   - Ben Thompson managed this for 11 years, but acknowledged "limitations"

**Mitigation Strategies:**

- **Embrace Asynchronous Work:**
  - Detailed written communication
  - Loom videos for explanations
  - Email-first culture
  - Accept that real-time sync is luxury, not requirement

- **Strategic Schedule Flexibility:**
  - Tim takes some calls during Thailand evening (US morning)
  - Blocks 1-2 evenings per week for US time zone work
  - Protects most of schedule for deep work during Thailand day

- **Distributed Team:**
  - Hire US-based spokesperson for media (can do live hits)
  - Europe-based researchers (time zone bridge between Asia and US)
  - Follow-the-sun model for coverage

**Realistic Assessment:**
For a **research and ratings organization**, time zone challenges are **manageable**. For a **breaking news outlet**, they would be severe. AI watchdog is closer to former.

### Legal Threats and SLAPP Suit Risks

**What Are SLAPP Suits?**

Strategic lawsuits against public participation—lawsuits intended to censor, intimidate, and silence critics through cost of legal defense. **Plaintiffs don't expect to win; goal is to burden defendant until they stop criticism.**

**Scale of Problem:**

- 50%+ of SLAPPs involve defamation claims
- Media Defence supported 188 cases in 2022, 40% were SLAPPs
- Rich and powerful pursue SLAPPs against smaller orgs, journalists, NGOs
- Legal fees can bankrupt defendants even if claim is meritless

**Notable Cases:**

- **Daphne Caruana Galizia (Malta):** Faced 47 libel lawsuits before assassination in 2017
- **Polish local media:** 187 legal complaints, 66 SLAPPs between 2015-2021
- **Investigative journalists globally:** Media Defence supports cases in 23 countries

**US Anti-SLAPP Protections:**

- **33 states + DC + Guam have anti-SLAPP laws** (as of 2023)
- Allows early dismissal of meritless lawsuits
- Defendant can recover legal fees if suit deemed SLAPP
- **Important:** Strength varies by state; California and Texas have strong anti-SLAPP laws

**International Dimension:**

- **EU Directive (2024):** Adopted protections against SLAPPs
- **UK activism:** Coalition including English PEN, Index on Censorship, NUJ pushing for stronger laws
- **Thailand:** No specific anti-SLAPP protections (potential vulnerability)

- **Implication:** Some protection, but don't rely on it

**4. Neutral Reportage:**

- **Implication:** If reporting others' findings, clearly attribute

  - Don't rely on insider scoops (Consumer Reports model)
  - Base ratings on public testing and documentation
  - Whistleblower-friendly infrastructure (SecureDrop, encrypted comms)
  - **Frame as advantage:** "We're independent because we're not embedded"
- **Assessment:** Less critical for ratings org than investigative journalism

**6. Policy Influence Limitations:**
- **Risk Level:** MEDIUM
- **Description:** US policymakers don't take Thailand-based org seriously
- **Mitigation:**
  - Partner with US-based advocacy organizations (EFF, AI Now)
  - Hire DC-based policy advisor (when scaling)
  - Publish research that policymakers cite (regardless of org location)
  - Travel for key Congressional briefings
- **Precedent:** International orgs (Transparency International, Reporters Without Borders) successfully influence US policy

### Legal Risks

**7. Defamation Lawsuits (US Jurisdiction):**
- **Risk Level:** MEDIUM-HIGH (Comes with the territory)
- **Description:** AI company sues for negative rating
- **Mitigation:**
  - Incorporate as US 501(c)(3) (anti-SLAPP protections)
  - Media liability insurance ($5K/year)
  - Legal retainer with media law firm
  - Rigorous fact-checking and methodology
  - Clear opinion labeling
- **Assessment:** Manageable with proper legal structure and insurance

**8. SLAPP Suits (Intimidation Tactics):**
- **Risk Level:** MEDIUM
- **Description:** Company files meritless suit to drain resources
- **Mitigation:**
  - Anti-SLAPP laws (incorporate in CA or TX)
  - Legal support from nonprofits (Media Defence, Reporters Shield)
  - Public campaign against SLAPP (increases reputational cost for plaintiff)
  - Crowdfunding legal defense if needed
- **Assessment:** Real risk for adversarial watchdog; must prepare

**9. Thai Legal Exposure:**
- **Risk Level:** LOW-MEDIUM
- **Description:** If AI company operates in Thailand, could sue under Thai law
- **Mitigation:**
  - Operate under US entity (not Thai business)
  - Don't publish content specifically targeting Thai market (focus on US/global)
  - Understand Thai Computer Crime Act constraints
  - Legal counsel in Thailand if risk escalates
- **Assessment:** Unlikely given focus on US AI companies, but monitor

**10. International Jurisdiction Complexity:**
- **Risk Level:** LOW
- **Description:** Lawsuits in multiple jurisdictions simultaneously
- **Mitigation:**
  - Strong US legal entity structure
  - International media law experts on retainer
  - Insurance with international coverage
- **Assessment:** Low probability; most companies would sue in US

### Financial Risks

**11. Funding Restrictions (Geographic):**
- **Risk Level:** MEDIUM
- **Description:** Some US foundations only fund US-based 501(c)(3)s with US operations
- **Mitigation:**
  - Form US 501(c)(3) (solves most of this)
  - Have US board members and registered agent
  - Demonstrate US activities (even if remote team)
  - Focus on unrestricted individual donations initially
- **Assessment:** Mitigated by US entity structure

**12. Runway Miscalculation:**
- **Risk Level:** MEDIUM
- **Description:** Thailand cost savings lead to underestimating growth capital needs
- **Mitigation:**
  - Conservative budgeting
  - Model scenarios (Thailand low-burn vs US scaling)
  - Understand that US expansion requires capital
  - Plan fundraising milestones
- **Assessment:** Standard startup risk; Thailand extends runway but doesn't eliminate need for revenue

### Geopolitical Risks

**13. US-China Tensions:**
- **Risk Level:** LOW-MEDIUM
- **Description:** Coverage of Chinese AI companies creates political complications
- **Mitigation:**

  - Don't position as "US vs China" framing
  - Thailand neutrality is advantage (not aligned with either)
- **Assessment:** Could become advantage if positioned as neutral global watchdog

**14. Thailand Political Instability:**

### Reputation Risks

**15. Capture / Bias Accusations:**
- **Risk Level:** MEDIUM (for any watchdog)

**16. Methodology Challenges:**
- **Risk Level:** HIGH (Core to credibility)

- **Mitigation:**
  - **Academic rigor:** Peer-reviewed approach

  - **Public testing:** Invite third parties to replicate results
  - **Transparent limitations:** Acknowledge what ratings can/can't measure
- **Assessment:** This is THE critical risk—methodology must be bulletproof

### My Specific Recommendation for Tim

**START IN THAILAND. Here's why:**

**1. Financial Prudence:**
You're bootstrapping. Thailand gives you 2-3 years instead of 1 year. This is the difference between:
- Having time to develop rigorous methodology
- Rushing to market with half-baked ratings to generate revenue

Consumer Reports' credibility comes from **methodological rigor**. You can't build that under financial pressure.

**2. Methodological Development:**
Year 1 is about **creating the rating system**, not networking. You need:
- Deep focus time (Thailand's pace supports this)
- Iteration without public pressure
- Ability to fail privately and improve
- Ben Thompson's insight: Taiwan was "optimized for one-to-many email"—similarly, Thailand is optimized for research/development phase

**3. Differentiation:**
Starting from Thailand immediately signals:
- "We're not Silicon Valley insiders"
- "We cover AI globally (including China)"
- "We're independent by structure and geography"

This is a **feature, not a bug** for a watchdog organization.

**4. De-Risking:**
If AI watchdog doesn't work, you've spent 1-2 years in Thailand with low burn rate.
If it launches from San Francisco and fails, you've burned through capital and created financial stress.

**Thailand allows experimentation without catastrophic downside.**

**5. Built-In Escalation Path:**
You can always move to the US later (like Ben Thompson did).
You cannot easily move back to Thailand while maintaining US operations.
**Start lean, scale strategically.**

**WHEN TO CONSIDER RELOCATING TO US:**

**Trigger Signals:**
1. **Revenue plateaus** due to US presence perception (e.g., hitting $300K and unable to reach $500K)
2. **Major foundation grants** requiring US-based staff
3. **Policy influence** becomes larger part of mission (Congressional testimony, regulatory engagement)
4. **Personal factors** (family, quality of life, etc.)—like Ben Thompson's daughter's college

**Don't relocate if:**
- Still in research/development phase
- Revenue growing steadily from Thailand
- Remote model working well
- Team distributed anyway (US office wouldn't centralize)

**Ben Thompson's Lesson:**
He built $3M/year business from Taiwan before relocating. **Prove the model remotely first.** Then, if specific opportunities require US presence, you have the revenue to support it.
